Step 1 · Getting started

Know your students. Know your system.

Before anything else, special educators need a clear picture of who they serve and how support is organized in their district. That means understanding demographics, programs, and the legal protections that shape your work.

First-week checklist

Mindset

Any student can succeed with the right supports. Your role is to move them from “what is” to “what could be” by matching services, scaffolds, and expectations to their needs.

Step 2 · Legal foundations

IEPs, 504s, and your responsibilities

Every special educator must understand the difference between an Individualized Education Program (IEP) and a 504 Plan, as well as the legal requirements around implementation and documentation.

IEP: Special education services under IDEA. Provides specialized instruction, goals, related services, and accommodations.

504 Plan: Access plan under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. Provides accommodations and supports so the student can access the general education program.

In New York, teachers and service providers who work with a student with an IEP are required to receive and acknowledge access to the plan. Practically, that means:

  • Reading each student’s current IEP.
  • Signing any required acknowledgement forms.
  • Checking back after annual reviews or amendments.

Quick implementation tips

  • Build a simple accommodation tracker for each class you support.
  • Flag any accommodations that are not being used so you can problem-solve with the team.
  • Document how you are implementing supports—especially for testing and major assessments.
Step 3 · Month-by-month

Your year at a glance

Special education work follows a rhythm: fall setup, winter meetings and evaluations, spring transition planning. Use this timeline as a loose roadmap and adapt it to your district’s calendar.

September · Foundations

  • Acknowledge all IEPs and 504s on your roster.
  • Send welcome letters to families and students.
  • Create accommodation quick-reference sheets for each class.
  • Set up digital folders/logs for each primary student.

October · Assessments & consent

  • Verify consent to test for students due for reevaluations.
  • Administer Level 1 transition assessments by grade band.
  • Identify students who need WIAT-IV or other formal testing.
  • Apply for College Board/ACT testing accommodations for eligible students.

November–December · Meetings & progress

  • Send teacher report forms 4–6 weeks before meetings.
  • Compile meeting packets (reports, Level 1s, testing, draft projections).
  • Complete and mail quarterly progress reports aligned to IEP goals.

January–June · Transition & scheduling

  • Draft projections and program recommendations for the coming year.
  • Confirm course selections align with diploma options and graduation requirements.
  • Complete exit summaries for seniors and students aging out.
Step 4 · Assessment & progress

Academic assessment & progress monitoring

Evaluations and day-to-day data are how we show that services are appropriate and effective. Use formal tools (like the WIAT-IV) alongside ongoing classroom data.

WIAT-IV overview

  • Reading: Word Reading, Pseudoword Decoding, Reading Comprehension.
  • Math: Numerical Operations, Math Problem Solving.
  • Writing: Spelling, Sentence Composition, Essay Composition.
  • Oral Language: Listening Comprehension, Oral Expression.

Progress monitoring ideas

  • Curriculum-based measures (CBM) for reading fluency, computation, or writing.
  • Short teacher-made probes aligned to specific IEP goals.
  • Data from digital learning platforms.
  • Brief observational notes tied to behavior or executive-function goals.
